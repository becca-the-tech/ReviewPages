Day 3- 6/24/2020

The past few weeks I've been reviewing my skills and trying to get job ready. Today I read from Ultralearning and I worked on the into to SQL for Data Science on EdX, which I have until July 12th to finish working on. The SQL course is cool because it has students make a IBM cloud D2 instance using a free account, which we'll add a data set to in order to practice our SQL skills.

The part of Ultralearning I read today made these points:

  • Experiment with your learning: pick something and stay focused on it exclusively for a length of time
  • Periodically review if you're on the right track or not and reassess goals
  • Beginners learn all at the same level, nothing.
  • As learning progresses, skillsets diverge and more options exist, leading to difficulty picking a choice

Day 2- 5/31/2020

Today, I mostly relaxed and tried to create a realistic guide for my month of June, telling me what to focus on for improvement. I read a lot from Cal Newport's tips for Straight A Students (1), along with reading some of Ultralearning (2).

Some tips from those readings:

  • Prioritize assignments instead of reading it all (1)
  • For non-technical classes, focus on the overall picture using question/evidence/conclusion (1)
  • For technical classes, write down examples and solutions, but not necessarily every step in between (1)
  • Drill particular lessons that you find most difficult, before bringing them back into the project context of whatever you're working on (2)
  • The best type of reviewing is free recall without looking at notes, to see how much you remember (2)

Day 1- 5/30/2020

This week I've been watching a conference called Empower to Connect as part of my church's training for those who volunteer with kids. I love volunteering with kids and have been since June of 2018, so this training was a great supplement to my hands-on experience.

This training discussed TBRI (Trust-Based Relational Intervention), and how we can help kids who come from difficult circumstances. Here are some of the lessons I learned from the conference:

  • Reassure the child you love them no matter what and aren't going to leave
  • When dealing with difficult behavior, make sure you distinguish that you're saying no to the behavior rather than the child
  • Focus on seeing the need behind the acting out behavior-- often this is because they're in survival mode
  • We (adults) need to be the role models and teach kids more acceptable behavior to replace their coping strategies
